Equations

These calculators are based entirely on the equations presented in this study. Here I describe how I've adapted them to get the results above.

The study measures the maximal vertical force produced by a group of climbers with 3 different grip types (half crimp, full crimp, slope a.k.a. 4 finger open hand) on 10mm, 20mm, 30mm, and 40mm edges. The methodology is better explained in the study itself, but basically the climbers pulled on a force sensor for each grip/edge combo and researchers recorded the maximal force. They graphed the points and did some curve fitting and obtained the following polynomials:

Functions (f(x))
half crimp$$-2.92\cdot{ x}^{4}+36.43\cdot{ x}^{3}+-194.6\cdot{ x}^{2}+512\cdot x$$
full crimp$$-6.83\cdot{ x}^{4}+79.72\cdot{ x}^{3}+-357.8\cdot{ x}^{2}+724.5\cdot x$$
slope crimp$$-9.74\cdot{ x}^{4}+98.97\cdot{ x}^{3}+-362.7\cdot{ x}^{2}+634.2\cdot x$$

You plug in x, the depth of your edge in cm, and out comes the expected force you can generate on that edge, in newtons. Centimeters and newtons aren't really units that climbers think in, though, so I converted the units into something more useful. I can take x in millimeters and convert to cm before I plug it in, and I can take the output force in newtons, and divide it by the absolute maximum of the polynomial, to get a percentage of the max force (e.g. 2% = 0.02). Now you plug in edge depth in mm, and get an expected percentage of your max weight that you can hang, and the equations look like so:

Scaled functions (fscaled(x))
half crimp$$\frac{-2.92\cdot\left({\frac{ x}{10}}\right)^{4}+36.43\cdot\left({\frac{ x}{10}}\right)^{3}+-194.6\cdot\left({\frac{ x}{10}}\right)^{2}+512\cdot\frac{ x}{10}}{533.34}$$
full crimp$$\frac{-6.83\cdot\left({\frac{ x}{10}}\right)^{4}+79.72\cdot\left({\frac{ x}{10}}\right)^{3}+-357.8\cdot\left({\frac{ x}{10}}\right)^{2}+724.5\cdot\frac{ x}{10}}{554.021}$$
slope crimp$$\frac{-9.74\cdot\left({\frac{ x}{10}}\right)^{4}+98.97\cdot\left({\frac{ x}{10}}\right)^{3}+-362.7\cdot\left({\frac{ x}{10}}\right)^{2}+634.2\cdot\frac{ x}{10}}{574.486}$$

I wanted the answers to these questions:
 What percentage of my max weight can I hang on an x mm edge?
 If I can hang x total weight on a y mm edge, how much weight total can I hang on a z mm edge?
 If I can hang x total weight on a y mm edge, what is the maximum total weight I can hang, and on what edge size?
 If I can hang x total weight on a y mm edge, on what size edge (mm) can I hang z total weight?
 If I can hang with x weight added to y bodyweight on a z mm edge, what is the smallest edge I can hang at just bodyweight?

What percentage of my max weight can I hang on an x mm edge?

For this, I plug the edge depth into any of the scaled functions above. Example, for edge depth 18mm: $$percentage=\frac{-2.92\cdot\left({\frac{ x}{10}}\right)^{4}+36.43\cdot\left({\frac{ x}{10}}\right)^{3}+-194.6\cdot\left({\frac{ x}{10}}\right)^{2}+512\cdot\frac{ x}{10}}{533.34}$$ $$percentage=\frac{-2.92\cdot\left({\frac{18}{10}}\right)^{4}+36.43\cdot\left({\frac{18}{10}}\right)^{3}+-194.6\cdot\left({\frac{18}{10}}\right)^{2}+512\cdot\frac{18}{10}}{533.34}$$ $$percentage = 0.89\space or\space 89\%$$

If I can hang x total weight on a y mm edge, how much weight total can I hang on a z mm edge?

To find the maximal weight:
This is the half crimp equation, but fscaled(x) could be any of the scaled functions. $$f_{scaled}(x)=\frac{-2.92\cdot\left({\frac{ x}{10}}\right)^{4}+36.43\cdot\left({\frac{ x}{10}}\right)^{3}+-194.6\cdot\left({\frac{ x}{10}}\right)^{2}+512\cdot\frac{ x}{10}}{533.34}$$ $$f_{scaled}(edge)=percent$$ $$weight = max*percent$$ $$\frac{weight}{percent} = max$$ $$\frac{weight}{f_{scaled}(edge)}=max$$ Knowing this, the weight k you could hang on an arbitrary edge of j mm is $$k=f_{scaled}(j)*max$$ $$k=f_{scaled}(j)*\frac{y}{f_{scaled}(x)}$$ So: if you can hang weight y on x mm edge, you can hang weight k on j mm edge.

If I can hang x total weight on a y mm edge, what is the maximum total weight I can hang, and on what edge size?

I found this in the last calculation: $$\frac{weight}{f_{scaled}(edge)}=max$$

The optimal edge size doesn't vary at all; it's just the x-coordinate of the absolute maximum of each function. I don't have any elaboration here; I used an online calculator to find each maximum. For half crimp, optimal edge is 32.6068mm, full crimp 26.1901mm, and slope 39.5151mm.

If I can hang with x weight added to y bodyweight on a z mm edge, what is the smallest edge I can hang at just bodyweight?

I know: $$total \space weight = weight + bodyweight$$ $$\frac{total \space weight}{f_{scaled}(edge)}=max$$ $$bodyweight \div max = j$$ j is the percent of max that bodyweight is; bodyweight is j percent of max.
fscaled(x) takes an edge depth in mm as input and outputs a percentage. I need to find its inverse f'scaled(x), which takes a percentage and ouputs an edge depth.

If I find f'(x), it's trivial to find f'scaled(x), so that's what I'll do.
